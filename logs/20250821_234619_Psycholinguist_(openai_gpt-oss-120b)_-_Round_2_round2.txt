## 1. Coercive Control & Surveillance  
**Label:** *Controllo coercitivo e sorveglianza*  

| # | Item (original wording) |
|---|--------------------------|
| 1 | *Mi sento controllata, ho la sensazione che non si fidi di me e che voglia sapere sempre dove sono e con chi…* |
| 4 | *Sento che mi manda troppi messaggi o mi chiama in modo insistente* |

| 21 | *Controlla come spendo i miei soldi* |

| 44 | *Mi viene chiesto di giustificare ogni acquisto che faccio* |
| 45 | *Diventa estremamente geloso o possessivo* |

**Rationale** – These statements all describe behaviours that restrict the victim’s autonomy by **monitoring** whereabouts, communications and personal choices **and** by **using economic levers** (access to money, spending control, employment sabotage) to create dependency.  The demand for “proof of love” and the extreme jealousy are classic tactics that reinforce surveillance, so they are best placed together with the other monitoring items rather than in a separate “emotional‑abuse” factor.  By merging the financial‑control items with the behavioural‑surveillance items we obtain a single, theoretically coherent “coercive control” dimension that mirrors contemporary IPV taxonomies (e.g., Stark 2007; WHO 2021).

Item 31, which in the first version was placed under “behavioural control”, is better understood as a demand for exclusive time that serves the same purpose of limiting alternative social contacts.  Grouping these items yields a clean “isolation” factor that is widely recognised in intimate‑partner‑violence research.

The dimension captures the “fear‑inducing” side of coercive control that is not captured by the purely behavioural‑surveillance items.
