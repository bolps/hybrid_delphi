This prevents overlap with the broader coercive‑control factor.  

**Merging / separating dimensions**  
* The original nine‑factor solution split “Emotional Abuse” into several sub‑scales (e.g., “emotional unpredictability”, “relational safety”). The Delphi synthesis kept “Emotional Abuse” as a single factor and introduced a separate “Emotional Unpredictability & Gas‑lighting” factor. We therefore:

  * Pull the two gas‑‑lighting statements into a dedicated **Emotional Unpredictability & Gas‑lighting** factor (Factor 7).  
  * Group the “silent‑treatment”, “fear of anger”, and “lack of support” items into a new **Erosion of Relational Safety** factor (Factor 8). * Economic abuse was a stand‑alone factor in the Delphi list; we kept it (Factor 6) but limited it to the two items that directly block financial access or employment, leaving the “justifying purchases” items in the coercive‑control factor where they conceptually belong (monitoring of spending).  

* Physical violence and threats were merged in the Delphi “Threats, Intimidation, and Physical Violence” dimension; we followed that pattern (Factor 4).  

**Item allocation**  
* No item appears in more than one factor. Duplicate wording that appeared both in the “control” and “isolation” sections of the original list was kept only once and placed in the isolation factor (Factor 3).  
* All 41 statements are accounted for; the reverse‑coded item‑41 is listed separately as Factor 9.  

**Why the new eight‑factor model is psychometrically sensible**  

1. **Within‑factor unidimensionality** – Items in each factor share a common behavioural or psychological mechanism (e.g., surveillance, devaluation, isolation).  
2. **Between‑factor discriminability** – Each factor taps a qualitatively different abuse strategy, minimizing cross‑loadings (e.g., financial‑monitoring items load on coercive control, whereas outright denial of access loads on economic abuse).  
3. **Theoretical coherence** – The structure mirrors the contemporary “coercive‑control” model (which distinguishes control, emotional abuse, physical/sexual aggression, economic abuse, and gas‑lighting) while also honouring the Delphi‑identified isolation and relational‑safety dimensions.  
4. **Practical utility** – Sub‑scale scores can be computed by summing the items in each factor; the reverse‑coded item can be used to flag respondents who endorse a more positive relational perception, providing a built‑in validity check.
