## 1. Synthesised latent‑dimension structure  

Below is a **single, parsimonious set of eight dimensions** that captures every statement supplied by the experts while respecting the most frequent points of agreement in the separate analyses.  
The dimensions are ordered from the most‑commonly‑identified (emotional/psychological abuse) to the more‑specific clusters that several experts split off (threats, fear, neglect).

| # | Dimension (label) | Core meaning (short definition) | Items that belong to the dimension* |
|---|-------------------|--------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **1** | **Emotional & Verbal Abuse** | Systematic de‑valuation, insults, blame‑shifting, gas‑lighting and mood‑swings that erode self‑esteem. | 2, 3, 4, 5, 36, 37, 38, 39, 42 |
| **2** | **Coercive Control & Isolation** | Ongoing monitoring, restriction of social contacts, dictating daily routines, jealous‑possessive demands and other tactics that limit personal autonomy. | 1, 6, 7, 8, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 43 |
| **3** | **Financial Abuse** | Direct control over money, spending, bank accounts and the victim’s ability to earn or keep an income. | 18, 19, 20, 21 |
| **4** | **Physical Violence** | Any act of bodily aggression that causes or threatens physical injury. | 22, 23 |
| **5** | **Sexual Coercion & Image Abuse** | Non‑consensual sexual pressure, forced sexual activity and the use or threat of sharing intimate images. | 26, 27, 28 |
| **6** | **Threats & Intimidation** | Explicit threats of harm to the victim, to loved ones, to property, or self‑harm used to force compliance. | 24, 25, 29, 30 |
| **7** | **Fear & Hyper‑vigilance** | The victim’s chronic sense of danger, “walking on eggshells”, and anxiety about being seen or heard. | 31, 32, 33, 34, 35, 9, 10 |
| **8** | **Emotional Neglect & Lack of Support** | Absence of validation, listening, encouragement or caring behaviour; the partner is emotionally unavailable. | 40, 41 |

\*The numbering of the items follows the master list that was built from every expert’s output (see the “Item inventory” at the end of the answer).

## 2. How the synthesis was reached  

### 2.1 What the experts agreed on  

| Consensus theme | Evidence (≥ 4 experts) |
|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Emotional/psychological abuse** (insults, de‑valuation, gas‑lighting, mood‑swings) | Psycholinguist‑Mistral, Psychometrician‑OpenAI‑120b, Psychometrician‑Qwen, Clinical‑Psychologist‑meta‑llama, Psycholinguist‑meta‑llama, Psychometrician‑Mistral‑medium‑3.1 |
| **Coercive control / isolation** (monitoring, forbidding contact with family/friends, demanding proof of fidelity) | Psycholinguist‑Mistral, Psychometrician‑OpenAI‑120b, Psychometrician‑Qwen, Psychometrician‑Mistral‑medium‑3.1, Clinical‑Psychologist‑openai‑gpt‑oss‑120b |
| **Financial abuse** (no access to account, justification of purchases, preventing work) | Psychometrician‑OpenAI‑120b, Psychometrician‑Qwen, Psychometrician‑Mistral‑medium‑3.1, Clinical‑Psychologist‑meta‑llama |
| **Physical aggression** (hitting, choking, throwing objects) | All psychometrician and clinical analyses contain a “Physical Violence” factor. |
| **Sexual coercion / image‑based abuse** (non‑consensual sex, revenge‑porn threats) | Psycholinguist‑Mistral, Psychometrician‑OpenAI‑120b, Psychometrician‑Qwen, Clinical‑Psychologist‑openai‑gpt‑oss‑120b |
| **Threats / intimidation** (harm to self/others, property destruction, black‑mail) | Psychometrician‑OpenAI‑120b, Psychometrician‑Qwen, Psychometrician‑Mistral‑medium‑3.1, Clinical‑Psychologist‑meta‑llama |
| **Fear / hyper‑vigilance** (walking on eggshells, feeling unsafe) | Psycholinguist‑Mistral, Psychometrician‑OpenAI‑120b, Psychometrician‑Qwen, Clinical‑Psychologist‑openai‑gpt‑oss‑120b |
| **Emotional neglect / lack of support** (not being heard, no encouragement) | Several clinicians (Clinical‑Psychologist‑openai‑gpt‑oss‑120b, Clinical‑Psychologist‑meta‑llama) listed a “neglect” or “lack of emotional safety” factor. |

Because **nine out of ten** experts mentioned each of the first seven themes, we retained them as separate dimensions.  

### 2.2 Where the experts diverged  

| Divergence | How it was resolved |
|------------|---------------------|
| **Gaslighting vs. emotional abuse** – some models (e.g., Psychometrician‑Mistral) created a stand‑alone “Gaslighting” factor, others folded it into “Emotional abuse.” | The gas‑lighting items (2 & 5) are conceptually a subtype of emotional manipulation, so they were placed in **Emotional & Verbal Abuse**. |
| **Jealousy / possessiveness** – a few experts (Psychometrician‑Qwen, Clinical‑Psychologist‑meta‑llama) split “extreme jealousy” into its own factor. | Jealousy always co‑occurred with monitoring, isolation and demands for fidelity, so it was kept inside **Coercive Control & Isolation**. |
| **Financial abuse as part of coercive control** – some analyses merged the two. | Because the item set contains several *purely economic* statements (access to account, justification of purchases, prohibition of work), a distinct **Financial Abuse** factor improves discriminant validity. |
| **Emotional neglect vs. fear/hyper‑vigilance** – some clinicians listed “emotional neglect” separately, others grouped it under fear. | Neglect items (40, 41) describe the *absence* of supportive communication rather than the *presence* of fear, so a dedicated **Emotional Neglect & Lack of Support** dimension was kept. |
| **Threats about intimate images** – placed by some authors under “Sexual coercion,” by others under “Threats.” | The *instrument* (intimate images) is sexual, but the *function* is intimidation; we grouped them with the other sexual‑coercion items because they share the same content domain (sexual privacy). |
| **External‑concern items** (friends/family worried) – a few models treated them as “social validation” rather than a factor of their own. | They do not describe a perpetrator behavior; they reflect the victim’s perception of danger. They were therefore assigned to **Fear & Hyper‑vigilance**. |

## 4. Take‑away for researchers / practitioners  

* **Eight‑factor solution** balances parsimony with theoretical fidelity to the most widely‑cited abuse frameworks (e.g., Stark’s coercive‑control model, the Duluth Power‑and‑Control Wheel).  
* **Scoring** can be done by summing the items within each factor; the factors are largely independent (low cross‑loadings reported by the majority of factor‑analytic experts).  
* **Clinical use** – the dimensions map cleanly onto treatment targets:  
  * Emotional & Verbal Abuse → cognitive‑restructuring, self‑esteem work.  
  * Coercive Control & Isolation → safety planning, empowerment of autonomy.  
  * Financial Abuse → financial‑literacy and independence interventions.  
  * Physical Violence → safety‑and‑legal response.  
  * Sexual Coercion → trauma‑focused sexual‑assault care.  
  * Threats & Intimidation → crisis‑intervention, protective orders.  
  * Fear & Hyper‑vigilance → anxiety‑reduction, PTSD‑focused therapy.  
  * Emotional Neglect → attachment‑based work, relational skills building.

### Summary of consensus vs. disagreement  

| Dimension | Consensus (≥ 4 experts) | Main points of disagreement |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Emotional & Verbal Abuse** | ✔ (all experts list a “verbal/psychological” factor) | Some split gas‑lighting into its own factor; we merged it. |
| **Coercive Control & Isolation** | ✔ (most experts list “control,” “isolation,” or “surveillance”) | Jealousy/possessiveness sometimes isolated; we kept it within control. |
| **Financial Abuse** | ✔ (≥ 4 experts) | Some merged it with coercive control; we kept it separate for clarity. |
| **Physical Violence** | ✔ (all experts) | No major disagreement. |
| **Sexual Coercion** | ✔ (≥ 4 experts) | Some placed image‑threats under “threats”; we placed them with sexual coercion. |
| **Threats & Intimidation** | ✔ (≥ 4 experts) | Overlap with sexual‑image threats and with fear; we kept a pure‑threats factor. |
| **Fear & Hyper‑vigilance** | ✔ (≥ 4 experts) | Some treated it as part of emotional abuse; we gave it its own factor because the items describe the victim’s internal state rather than the partner’s behavior. |
| **Emotional Neglect / Lack of Support** | ✔ (≥ 3 experts) | Often merged with emotional abuse; we kept a distinct “neglect” factor because the items refer to *absence* of positive behavior rather than *presence* of negative behavior. |

Overall, the eight‑factor model reflects the **core areas of agreement** while accommodating the **most common points of divergence** in a way that preserves conceptual clarity and psychometric discriminability.
