## What changed compared with the original seven‑factor solution  

| Original dimension | Delphi‑inspired revision | Reason for the change |
|--------------------|--------------------------|-----------------------|
| **Positive relational support & safety** | Kept unchanged as **Healthy Relational Dynamics** (Dim 7). | The Delphi synthesis confirmed the need for a distinct “positive” factor; no modification required. |
| **Psychological / emotional abuse** | Split into **Emotional Abuse & Manipulation** (Dim 2) **and** **Fear & Hyper‑vigilance** (Dim 5). | The Delphi panel distinguished the victim’s *subjective fear* (items about walking on eggs, hiding actions) from the more “cold” verbal/psychological manipulation. Keeping the two constructs separate improves discriminant validity. |
| **Controlling & isolating behaviours** | Merged with the three **Economic abuse** items to form **Coercive Control & Isolation** (Dim 1). | Controlling behaviours, financial domination, and isolation are conceptually part of a single “coercive control” construct (as defined in the literature on intimate‑partner violence). The Delphi synthesis grouped them together, and empirical work shows they load on a common factor. |
| **Economic abuse** | No longer a stand‑alone factor; its items are now in Dim 1. | Overlap with the broader control dimension makes a separate factor unnecessary; the items showed high cross‑loadings on the control factor in the Delphi’s pooled analysis. |
| **Sexual coercion & privacy violation** | Split: the *physical* sexual pressure item joins **Physical & Sexual Violence** (Dim 3) while the *image‑based* threats move to **Threats & Intimidation** (Dim 4). | Image‑based coercion is more akin to a threat/intimidation tactic than to overt sexual violence; the Delphi panel placed those items with other threat items. |
| **Physical violence & overt threats of harm** | Kept as **Physical & Sexual Violence** (Dim 3) **and** **Threats & Intimidation** (Dim 4). | Threats that do not involve direct physical contact (e.g., destroying property, self‑harm threats) form a distinct “intimidation” factor in the Delphi synthesis. |
| **Fear & external concern** | Split into two separate dimensions: **Fear & Hyper‑vigilance** (Dim 5) and **External Concern & Validation** (Dim 6). | The Delphi synthesis distinguished the victim’s internal fear from the external validation provided by friends/family, which improves the interpretability of the scale and matches findings that third‑party concern predicts help‑seeking independently of personal fear. |

### Additional minor adjustments  

* **Item “Mi viene chiesto di giustificare ogni acquisto che faccio”** (monitoring of spending) was not listed in the Delphi’s “Coercive Control” block, but it clearly reflects financial surveillance. It was therefore placed in **Coercive Control & Isolation** (Dim 1) to keep the economic‑control content within the broader control factor.  
* **Item “Quando faccio qualcosa che a lui non va bene poi non mi parla per giorni”** (silent‑treatment) remained in the **Emotional Abuse** factor because it represents a classic manipulative tactic (the “stone‑walling” form of emotional abuse) rather than a fear‑based avoidance behavior.  
* **Item “Mi sento giudicata …”** also stayed in **Emotional Abuse**, as it captures verbal devaluation rather than anticipatory fear.

## Why this final structure is preferable  

1. **Theoretical coherence** – The seven dimensions map onto the most widely accepted taxonomy of intimate‑partner violence (e.g., Johnson’s typology, the WHO’s “psychological, physical, sexual, economic, and controlling” categories) while also preserving a positive‑relationship factor that can serve as a protective‑factor indicator.  

2. **Empirical alignment** – The Delphi synthesis was based on pooled expert factor solutions; adopting its grouping respects the convergent evidence that items such as financial monitoring, jealousy, and isolation load together, whereas fear‑related items form a separate factor.  

3. **Parsimony without loss of nuance** – By merging economic abuse into coercive control and separating threat‑based intimidation from overt physical violence, we reduce redundancy (no overlapping factors) while still capturing distinct mechanisms (physical harm vs. psychological intimidation).  

4. **Practical utility** – The final set yields clear sub‑scale scores that can be used for (a) risk assessment (e.g., high scores on Coercive Control & Isolation, Threats & Intimidation, Physical & Sexual Violence), (b) monitoring change over time, and (c) evaluating protective factors (Healthy Relational Dynamics) and social‑network support (External Concern).  

---

**In short:** the revised seven‑factor model respects the Delphi moderator’s synthesis, refines the original solution where the evidence suggested a better fit, and provides a theoretically sound, psychometrically tidy framework for measuring the full spectrum of relational experiences—from the healthiest to the most dangerous.
