### Rationale for the Groupings  

* **Homogeneity of Mechanism** – Each factor groups items that share the *same functional mechanism* (e.g., “behavioural restriction” for Coercive Control, “verbal devaluation” for Emotional & Verbal Abuse).  
* **Actor‑Receiver Distinction** – Factors 1‑6 describe **perpetrator actions**; Factors 7‑8 describe **victim experiences** (fear, hyper‑vigilance, neglect).  This avoids mixing cause and effect in a single factor, a point raised by several psychometricians.  
* **Theoretical Alignment** – The eight dimensions map onto the most widely cited IPV frameworks (Stark’s coercive‑control model, the Duluth Power‑and‑Control Wheel, and contemporary models of economic and technology‑facilitated abuse).  
* **Empirical Convergence** – At least four of the ten expert panels independently identified each of these eight clusters; the Delphi moderator’s final synthesis reflects that convergence.

## Remaining Disagreements / Divergences  

| Issue | Points of Contention | Consensus Position (after Round 2) | Why the disagreement remains (if any) |
|-------|----------------------|-----------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Placement of image‑based threats (Item 28)** | Some psycholinguists argued it belongs with the generic “Threats” factor because the verb *“minaccia”* signals intimidation. | Placed in **Sexual Coercion & Image Abuse** (Factor 5). | The Delphi panel judged the *content domain* (sexual‑privacy violation) to outweigh the grammatical cue. The split improves clinical relevance (image‑based abuse is treated legally as a sexual offence). |
| **Inclusion of external‑concern items (9 & 10) in Fear** | A few psychometricians suggested they belong to a “Social‑validation” factor or to Coercive Control. | Integrated into **Fear & Hyper‑vigilance** (Factor 7). | These items do not describe a controlling behaviour; they are **victim‑centered indicators of perceived danger**, which aligns with trauma‑theory definitions of hyper‑vigilance. |
| **Item 43 (“non mi sent libera di vestirmi”)** | Some early drafts placed it under “Emotional Neglect”. | Assigned to **Coercive Control & Isolation** (Factor 2). | The item regulates the victim’s *behavioural autonomy* (clothing choice) rather than expressing a lack of emotional support, fitting the control‑restriction theme. |
| **Whether “Gas‑lighting” (Items 2 & 5) warrants a separate factor** | A minority of experts kept a stand‑alone “Gas‑lighting” factor. | Merged into **Emotional & Verbal Abuse** (Factor 1). | Gas‑lighting is a *sub‑type* of emotional manipulation; separating it creates a heterogeneous factor with only two items and reduces reliability. |
| **Number of factors (7 vs. 8)** | A few panels proposed a seven‑factor solution by merging Fear with Emotional Neglect. | Eight‑factor model retained. | Fear and Neglect are conceptually distinct (active threat vs. passive omission). Keeping them separate preserves discriminant validity and gives clinicians a clearer treatment map. |

*Overall*, after the second Delphi round **no substantive disagreement remains** about the *content* of each factor; the residual points above are merely matters of labeling or minor placement that were resolved in favour of the eight‑factor consensus.
